An Animal Crossing Data Story

Gender and Personality in Animal Crossing

Twenty years of villager design, examined through the lens of gender, species, and social archetypes.

Animal Crossing is a game about community. Basically, depending on the game, you move to either a small town or a vacant island, and you sort of stumble into becoming the central figure of the place. As you progress, you randomly run into new villagers, and you can befriend them!

But who ARE the villagers? Across nine mainline releases spanning two decades, Nintendo has made a roster of 480 whopping unique villagers. What one might not realize however is that there is a lot of intentionality behind the roster, with it having its own sociological makeup of sorts that even mimics some stereotypical patterns in the real world. Gender and personality have an effect on some villager types, but our focus is gonna shift to how Nintendo itself maintains the apparent structural elements that comprises the general holistic view of how these characters can be viewed as a microcosm of gendered bias that reflects assumptions about what kinds of beings belong in an idealized social world.

...Okay, maybe that's a little intense for the scope of this project, but let's try running with this lol. We will look at the data behind these patterns, and consider what they reveal about how Animal Crossing imagines social life.

Part One: Populus

The Roster Literally Started with a Bang

As we can see below, the first thing the data makes clear is that the franchise's roster was largely realized right off the bat. The original Japanese release, Doubutsu no Mori (2001), introduced 216 villagers — nearly 45% of the entire franchise roster. Remember that this was before the Western release of the series, which would be Animal Crossing (2002) on the Gamecube.

With this evidence, we can thus deduce, considering the following data, that the first game set a strong precedent that cleared the blueprint for the series roster.

Every subsequent game gives more curatorial energy than anything else. Animal Crossing: New Leaf (2012) is the game that had most shaken up the case since the original release, adding an impressive 100 new characters, with 84 from Animal Crossing e+ following that, but even they didn't do much to the makeup of the roster.

A very interesting statistic here is that Animal Crossing: New Horizons (2020), the most commercially successful entry in the franchise, only introduced 8 new villagers. That should signal the strength of the core roster. Gender and personality makeup, as we will explore more below, have generally persisted since 2001.

Section Two: Gender

The Animal You Are Influences Your Gender

Amongst the official 480 villagers, the gender split is nearly even: roughly 230 male to 250 female. On the surface, this looks incredibly balanced, but it conceals asymmetries at the species level that, honestly, aren't nuanced at all. Even more so is the notion that the data shows these haven't shifted meaningfully across any game in the franchise.

First, let's lay some groundwork to show gender distribution of new villagers per game.

Gender makeup of new villagers per game, 2001–2020

As we can see here, each game is shockingly egalitarian about gender makeup of new characters. Keep this in the back of your mind as you move onward.

This next graph is quite enlightening...

Gender makeup of villagers species

Three species are entirely gender-locked. Every Bull and Lion villager is male, while every Cow villager is female. These quite intentional, as we can surmise that they may map directly onto gender associations humans have long projected onto these animals in the real world from a socioculturalist critical lens.

Animal Crossing didn't invent these associations; rather, it simply inherited and formalized them. The bull and lion are often depicted and romanticized as symbols, or cultural artifacts if you will, of masculinity, whereas the cow in an adverse sense is moreso associated with the opposite end historically. If there wasn't Some type of precedent being set, why haven't we had a slick lioness villager? Food for thought...

Other species skew heavily without being absolute. Eagles are 80% male. Kangaroos are 82% female, potentially stemming from the kangaroo's cultural perception of coded materns carrying a joey. Squirrels run 71% female. The category shapes were set in Doubutsu no Mori and no subsequent design team has chosen to challenge them.

Section Three: Personality

Your Personality Determines Your Gender

The most revealing pattern in the data isn't about species at all. It's about personality. Animal Crossing assigns each villager one of eight personality types. Across all 480 villagers, every personality is assigned exclusively to one gender. Not one villager crosses the line.

In a franchise celebrated for warmth and inclusivity, the full range of emotional and social expression available to any given character is determined entirely by their gender.

New Leaf makes this especially pointed. That game introduced both Smug and Big Sister as new personality types, reassigning previous villagers as such. And yet, they are still assigned along strict gender lines. The parallel is almost deliberate: as though the designers recognized they were building mirrors, choosing to keep them separated anyway.

New Horizons introduced exactly one villager of each personality type. Whether that's a quiet acknowledgment that the system had run its course, or simply routine roster management, the data in this aspect alone can't say. What it can and does say is that across two decades and nine games, no villager was ever designed to be a Lazy female or a Normal male. In Animal Crossing's social world, your emotional range is assigned at birth.

Closing Thoughts

What the Town Tells Us

Animal Crossing is not a game with malicious intent. It is, by every measure, and quite literally, one of the warmest and most beloved franchises in gaming. But warmth and rigidity can coexist, and the data suggests they have in this otherwise well-regarded and fan-cherished franchise for over twenty years.

It is worth noting mechanically that this surface-value difference is not at all doom and gloom. In fact, certain traits are practically the same besides gender assignment. For the sake of this study though, we treat these names semantically.

The question isn't whether Nintendo intended any of this. The question is what it means that a game about the joy of social connection imagined that social world through such a consistent, inflexible lens, and what it might look like to imagine it differently.

This 480-villager roster is a designed social world, and like all designed social worlds, it encodes the assumptions of its creators. Some of those assumptions — that cows are female, that lions are male, that, in personality design, women are nurturing and men are athletic — are so culturally familiar that they may read as natural without consideration of the stereotypes these traits persist. That familiarity is precisely what makes them worth examining and critiquing for there to be a more nuanced and less rigid social development cycle for the fun, caring, and otherwise dynamic collection of silly animal crossing villagers, at least one of whom will always liven up some player's day out there.
